HISTORY
The Oakland Fire Department was officially formed on March
13, 1869, with its first house, the Phoenix Engine Company No.1
located at Fifth and Washington Streets. As Oakland grew, OFD
absorbed neighboring volunteer departments. OFD evolved and grew
with the City into the diverse, skilled, courageous, and highly trained
Department that protects Oakland today.
OFD has constantly innovated and improved. The Department

purchased its first car, a Chief's vehicle, in 1908. The complete change
from horse drawn equipment to motor vehicles occurred by the early
1920s. Also in the 1920s, OFD became one of the first in the nation to
hire African American firefighters, desegregate in 1955, hire its first
Asian American firefighter in 1972, and its first woman in 1980.
As OFD has grown, new technologies and skills have been
adopted. OFD has developed teams and facilities to deal with all
disasters, including an Emergency Operations Center, Fire Dispatch
Center, Hazardous Materials Response team, Water Rescue team,
specialized BART operations, Heavy Rescue, Airport response,
Wildland firefighting, and Urban Search and Rescue. The full
capabilities of the OFD have been tested on numerous occasions. The
men and women of OFD have put their lives on the line to protect the
community during numerous major disasters in Oakland. These
disasters include the Caldecott Tunnel Fire of 1982, the Loma Prieta
Earthquake in 1989, and the Tunnel (or Hills fire) of 1991.
Today the courageous and diverse group of men and women
that make up the OFD serve the community with professionalism,
honor, and skill.
source “Oakland Fire Department” by BC Geoffrey Hunter (retired)

Oakland Fire Insignia
In the early days of North American fire
departments, orders were given to the troops, by
officers, using a large brass device that resembles

a megaphone, called “bugles” or speaking
trumpets. The person with the bugle hanging
from their neck or shoulder was easily identified
as the person in charge. Officers became
identified with these objects so a small pin in the
shape of a bugle became a type of rank insignia
for officers. The more “bugles on one’s collar” the
higher the rank. An expression still used today.

APX7000XE Radio
Turn Radio ON/OFF:
1. Turn the radio on by rotating the On/Off/Volume Control Knob clockwise
until you hear a click.
2. Turn the radio off by rotating the On/Off/Volume Control Knob
counterclockwise until you hear a click.

Select a Channel:
1. Rotate the 16-position Channel select knob to the desired channel. The
new channel name will appear on the display.
2. Press the PTT button to transmit. When speaking keep the microphone
1-2” directly in front of your mouth.
3. Release PTT button to listen

Select a Zone – Side Buttons or Menu
1. Press the right side of the 4-way Navigation button until Zone displayed.
2. Press the Menu Select button directly below Zone.
3. Press the Up or Down Navigation button to the required Zone. 4. Press
the Menu Select button directly below SEL to confirm displayed Zone.

Emergency – Trunked Feature (Top Orange Button)
1. With the radio turned on, press the emergency button on either the mic
or radio for 1 second.
The display alternates between showing “EMERGENCY” and the current
trunked zone and talkgroup/channel, and a short, medium-pitched emergency
tone sounds, white flashing light on RSM.
2. Dispatch will activate alert tone for 3 secons.
3. Press the PTT button and announce your emergency.
4. Release the PTT button to end the transmission and wait for a response
from the dispatcher.

Dispatch cannot clear an emergency. The radio user should NEVER
clear a true emergency on their radio without being instructed to do so
by dispatch.

KNOTS
▪ Knot a rope intertwined with itself, e.g. bowline, and figure 8. ▪
Bight a “U” shape in the rope.
▪ Loop is the rope forming a loop and continuing on. ▪
Running end describes the end actively being tied. ▪
Standing end describes the non-moving part.
Clove Hitch
Useful to quickly tie-off the end of a rope so it will not fall out of
your reach, and still easily adjustable. Known to slip and untie. Not
used as a tie-off or in place of an anchor knot, like the Figure 8.

Double Becket
Used for joining two unequal sized diameter material together. Easy
to tie, inspect, and untie after weighting with a rescue load.
